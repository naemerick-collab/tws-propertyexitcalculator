How we calculated these numbers

Tax on Sale

Adjusted basis = (Original purchase price + Capital improvements) − Accumulated depreciation.
Total gain = (Sale price − Selling costs) − Adjusted basis.
Depreciation recapture = min(Accumulated depreciation, Total gain) × 25% federal.
Capital gain = (Total gain − Recapture portion) × (Federal LTCG rate + NIIT if applicable + State rate).
Total tax = Recapture tax + Capital gain tax + State tax on full gain.

Prior 1031 Exchange (when toggle is on)

Carryover basis from a prior 1031 exchange is calculated as Original (relinquished) property purchase price + Capital improvements − Depreciation taken on the original property. This figure replaces "Original Purchase Price" in the adjusted-basis formula above. Cumulative depreciation taken across the original (relinquished) and replacement properties is included in the §1250 depreciation recapture calculation at projected sale. Annual depreciation on the replacement property is assumed to follow a fresh schedule based on the replacement property's cost; the carryover basis treatment for ongoing depreciation under Reg. §1.168(i)-6 may vary by election and should be confirmed with the client's tax advisor.

Hold (Status Quo)

No sale at Year 0, no tax event. Annual cashflow = (NOI grown at the NOI growth rate × (1 − capex reserve %)) − Debt service. When "Apply income tax to rental cashflow" is enabled, ordinary tax is applied to (net NOI − annual depreciation − mortgage interest). Wealth at exit = Property value (grown at appreciation rate) − Remaining mortgage (reduced by annual paydown). If "After-tax wealth at exit" is enabled, a hypothetical sale at horizon end is taxed (recapture + cap gains + NIIT + state); "Step-up in basis" zeroes that exit tax.

Sell & Pay Tax

Net cash to investor = (Sale price − Selling costs − Mortgage payoff − Total tax). Cash reinvested at the cash yield. Wealth at exit = After-tax cash compounded at the cash yield over the horizon.

Sell & Invest Passively

Same after-tax cash as the Sell & Pay Tax path, but invested at the higher target passive portfolio total return. Shown to compare an "exit real estate entirely" path.

1031 Exchange to DST

Tax fully deferred (assuming compliant exchange). Equity reinvested = (Sale price − Selling costs − Mortgage payoff) × (1 − DST load). Annual cashflow = Equity × DST cash-on-cash. Wealth at exit = Equity grown at DST appreciation. Tax deferred is not tax eliminated, basis carries over. When "After-tax wealth at exit" is enabled, exit tax is calculated on the gain over the carried-over basis from the original property. "Step-up in basis" zeroes that tax (a key estate-planning advantage of swap-til-you-drop).

Sell & Leveraged Bonus Depreciation

Modeled as a 2.0× (configurable) deduction multiplier: $1 invested in the LBD fund generates $2.00 of Year-1 passive loss. The exact multiplier depends on the specific fund's LTV and land allocation; typical range is 2.0–2.7× at current 100% bonus depreciation rates. Required LBD investment = Total gain ÷ Multiplier. The passive loss fully offsets the taxable gain at the federal level. Freed cash = Gross equity − LBD investment, which is then invested in the passive alternatives portfolio at the rate set in the Alternatives section. All deferred tax becomes due at the LBD fund's eventual disposition (when depreciation recapture applies). The LBD fund itself generates cash distributions and appreciation per the rates in the LBD section. Note on state conformity: states vary in how they treat federal bonus depreciation; this model assumes full conformity for simplicity, but actual state tax liability may differ in non-conforming states (CA, NY, NJ, MD, and others). Note on passive activity rules: the full passive-loss offset shown assumes the disposed-property gain qualifies as passive income. Real Estate Professionals (IRC §469(c)(7)) or investors with material participation should consult a tax advisor; the offset treatment may differ.

10-Year Projected Comparison Chart

The chart plots all five Detailed-Comparison strategies over a fixed 10-year horizon, in two switchable views, plus an optional S&P 500 market benchmark (off by default). Inputs come from the Benchmark Comparison Assumptions (S&P 500 return and dividend yield), the Alternative Portfolio Assumptions (passive total return and passive cash yield), the cash yield input (shared with the Sell & Pay Tax scenario), the DST inputs (appreciation, cash-on-cash yield, upfront load), the Leveraged Bonus Depreciation inputs (fund yield and appreciation, plus the offset/freed-cash split computed from your sale), and the property inputs for the Hold line (market value, appreciation, NOI growth, debt service). In both views, each strategy distributes its own annual cash flow, taken by the investor as income. DST, TWS Alts, and LBD distributions cannot be reinvested back into the fund and are always taken as income; Hold's rental income is likewise taken; Sell & Pay Tax (Cash) and S&P 500 income are also distributed (taken as income) by default, so the chart opens comparing every strategy's cash flow, but each can be switched to reinvest (compound) using the toggles above the chart.

How the chart maps to the Detailed Comparison table: the chart's six lines are: Hold Current Property, Sell & Pay Tax, Sell & Invest Passively, 1031 Exchange to DST, and Leveraged Bonus Depreciation (the five table strategies, on by default), plus the S&P 500 (a public-market benchmark with no table row, off by default). All five strategy lines use the same rate inputs, starting capital, and flat-distribution treatment as the matching table scenario. By default every strategy distributes its income, so the chart and the table agree out of the box. The two reinvest toggles let you optionally compound the Sell & Pay Tax and S&P 500 income instead, when turned on, those lines emphasize growth and show $0 distributed income, while the table still reports the cash flow each strategy would pay if taken as income. The underlying assumptions are identical; only the reinvest-vs-distribute presentation changes.

Projected equity over time view, plots each strategy's equity/principal at each year, with its own cash flow distributed as income (not reinvested into that vehicle unless noted):

Hold Current Property: No sale and no tax event, the line starts from your current equity (Market value − Mortgage). Equity(t) = Market value × (1 + appreciation)t − remaining mortgage (reduced by annual principal paydown). Rental income, NOI grown at the NOI-growth rate, less the capex reserve and debt service (and less rental income tax if enabled), is taken as income and grows over time.
Sell & Pay Tax (Cash): Starts from after-tax sale proceeds. By default interest (cash yield × principal) is taken as income and principal stays level; if "reinvest Sell & Pay Tax (cash yield)" is turned on, interest compounds at the cash yield instead. Same cash assumption and rate as the table's Sell & Pay Tax scenario.
Sell & Invest Passively (TWS Alts): Starts from after-tax sale proceeds; equity grows at (Passive total return − Passive cash yield) and a flat cash yield (After-tax proceeds × Passive cash yield) is distributed as income, alt-fund distributions cannot be reinvested into the fund.
1031 Exchange to DST: Reinvested equity = Gross equity × (1 − DST load). Equity grows on the full replacement-property value at the DST appreciation rate; the existing mortgage carries to the replacement property. Flat annual cash flow (Equity × DST cash-on-cash yield) is distributed, DST distributions cannot be reinvested into the trust. Equity(t) = Replacement value × (1 + DST appreciation)t − Replacement debt.
Leveraged Bonus Depreciation: Defers tax via bonus depreciation, so it deploys close to the full pre-tax proceeds, split between the LBD fund (grows at the bonus-depreciation appreciation rate) and freed cash invested in the alternatives portfolio (grows at Passive total return − Passive cash yield). Both sleeves distribute flat income (fund yield + freed-cash passive yield), taken by the investor.
S&P 500 (benchmark, off by default): Starts from after-tax sale proceeds. By default the dividend (S&P dividend yield × balance) is taken as income and principal grows at the price-appreciation rate (S&P total return − dividend yield); if "reinvest S&P 500 dividends" is turned on, no income is taken and principal compounds at the full S&P total return.

Income & growth split view, summarizes the same strategies at the 10-year horizon as a stacked bar: ending equity (the bottom segment, shown in each strategy's own color) plus the cumulative cash flow taken as income (the gold cap on top). A taller colored base means more was left to grow; a taller gold cap means more income was drawn over the decade. This makes the income-vs-growth trade-off explicit: higher-cash-flow strategies generally show a larger gold cash cap and a smaller colored growth base, and lower-cash-flow strategies the reverse. The bar total is the strategy's combined value delivered (income received + equity retained), before any tax on a future sale of the remaining equity.

Source & rationale for default benchmark assumptions: S&P 500 long-term real (inflation-adjusted) total return is approximately 6.5–7% per Damodaran/NYU Stern historical equity-return data and Ibbotson SBBI; nominal long-term average is ~10%. The 7% default is the conservative real-return figure for forward projections. S&P 500 dividend yield of ~1.8% reflects approximate trailing 12-month index yield. The cash yield used for the Cash line is the same input the Sell & Pay Tax scenario uses, a short-term cash / money-market rate (auto-populated from a 3-month Treasury proxy and adjustable), so the two surfaces always assume the same cash return.

Tax treatment in the chart: The 1031 line in both views assumes successful tax deferral and does not show a hypothetical after-tax exit value at year t (unlike earlier chart versions). Tax deferred is not tax eliminated, see the 1031 / DST Risk Disclosure below. The Sell & Pay Tax, Sell & Invest Passively, and S&P 500 lines all start from after-tax sale proceeds (Gross equity − Total tax computed from the inputs), so the upfront tax cost of selling is already reflected in their starting position. The Hold line has no sale, so it starts from your current (un-taxed) equity (Market value − Mortgage). The Leveraged Bonus Depreciation line defers the gain via bonus depreciation, so it starts from close to the full pre-tax proceeds (no immediate tax in the typical full-offset case), split between the fund and the freed cash. These different starting points are intentional, they reflect each strategy's real tax consequence at the moment of decision.

Oil & Gas Investment Risks

Private investments in oil and gas are high risk, including but not limited to, the following considerations: Political risk - Federal or local governments could enact regulations or legislation that could adversely affect the oil and gas industry, thereby negatively affecting your investment. Geographical risk - Oil and gas production can be negatively impacted by extraction challenges and the possibility that accessible reserves in a deposit may be smaller than estimated. There is no guarantee that any drilling operation will be successful. Supply demand and pricing risk - A reduction in oil and gas prices and/or governmental action can reduce or even eliminate investment returns. Discounted costs - Any dividend payments may be reduced or eliminated if the company is unable to generate sufficient revenue to fund payments to investors. Oil spill risk - Beyond the cost of repairs, cleanup, potential fines, and possible litigation, oil spills can damage the company's reputation, all of which can reduce or eliminate investment returns.

Opportunity Zone Disclosures

Investing in Opportunity Zones is speculative. Opportunity Zones are newly formed entities with no operating history. There is no assurance of investment return, property appreciation, or profits. The ability to meet the fund's underlying investment properties or businesses is not guaranteed. Investing in Opportunity Zone funds may involve higher risk than other established real estate offerings. Long-term investment - Opportunity Zone funds held require investors to maintain investments that may not be easily sold. The return of capital and realization of gains, if any, generally occur only upon partial or complete disposition or refinancing of such investments. Limited secondary market for Opportunity Zone - Although secondary markets may provide a limited liquidity option, the amount typically realized is often discounted from current valuations. Difficult valuation assessments - The portfolio holdings in Opportunity Zone funds may be challenging to value, as financial markets or exchanges usually do not quote or trade these holdings. Therefore, market prices for most of a fund's holdings are not readily available. Capital call default consequences - Meeting capital calls is a contractual obligation. Failure to fulfill this requirement in a timely manner could result in significant adverse consequences, including, but not limited to, forfeiture of your interest in the fund. Leverage - Opportunity Zone funds may use leverage in connection with real estate investments. Leverage creates a high degree of financial risk and may increase exposure to factors such as rising interest rates, economic downturns, or unanticipated investment. Regulation - Due to tax, regulatory, or investment decisions, it is possible that a fund or its investors may be unable to realize anticipated tax benefits. It is advisable to evaluate the merits of the underlying investment and not invest solely for potential tax advantages.
